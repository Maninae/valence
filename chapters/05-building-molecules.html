    5.1 Dots for the outermost

    Chemistry runs on valence electrons: the outer $s$ and $p$. Everything deeper is buried. Gilbert Lewis, 1916: write the symbol, draw one dot per valence electron, singly first, then pair.

    Columns are valence counts: alkali metals one, halogens seven. Group number, drawn.

    5.2 Eight is full

    Outer layer = one $s$ + one $p$: $1 + 3 = 4$ orbitals at two electrons each, so $4 \times 2 = 8$. That is "full," and why the nobles sit on the right edge.

    It's the lowest energy available, so atoms share or transfer until each counts eight. Hydrogen: shell 1 is just $1s$, full is two. A duet.

    The octet rule

    Main-group atoms bond so each counts a full outer shell: eight, or two for hydrogen.

    The trick: a shared pair counts for both atoms. A covalent bond, on paper.

    5.3 Lines are pairs

    One line = one shared pair = one bond. Pairs on one atom are lone pairs: two dots.

    Recipe: count valence electrons; least-electronegative atom centers (never H); draw singles; spread leftovers as lone pairs, outer atoms first; if any atom is short of eight, slide a lone pair into a gap for a double or triple.

    Worked example: water (H₂O)

    Oxygen 6, each H 1. Total 8. Oxygen centers. Two O–H bonds use 4; the other 4 are two lone pairs. Each H counts 2; oxygen counts $2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8$.

    Worked example: ammonia (NH₃)

    Nitrogen 5, each H 1. Total 8. Nitrogen centers. Three N–H bonds use 6; the last 2 are one lone pair. Each H counts 2; nitrogen counts $2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8$.

    Why it works

    Each click moves electrons between two accounts: shared or lone. The win condition is the octet rule on every atom. That every molecule has a valid answer is why the notation exists.

    5.5 Double and triple are shorter and stronger

    CO₂, O₂, and N₂ force doubles and triples: same bond, more pairs per gap. More glue: nuclei closer, harder to break. Carbon–carbon, in numbers:

    Triple bonds are why $\text{N}_2$ is inert: splitting it costs 945 kJ/mol, nearly three O–H bonds. Four-fifths of the air is N₂, and almost none reacts at room temperature. The atmosphere is held by a triple bond.

    5.6 What the dots can't say

    Lewis is flat bookkeeping: which atoms bond and how many pairs sit where. It doesn't say methane's four C–H bonds aim at tetrahedron corners, that water bends at about 105°, or that water sticks to itself through hydrogen bonds: why ice floats, DNA holds shape, blood is liquid at body temperature.

    Geometry and stickiness are behind the door the notation opened. The next site picks up there.

    Coming in v2

    VSEPR turns dots into 3D: each pair is a balloon shoving the others away. Polarity plus geometry sets net dipole, which sets what dissolves in water, which sets most of biology. Hydrogen bonding next.

    5.7 What you now have

    • Lewis dot symbols: valence count around the symbol, singly before pairing (Hund, in ink).
    • Octet (duet for H): one $s$ + three $p$, capped by Pauli, give an outer shell of eight: the noble-gas state.
    • A bond is a shared pair both atoms count. Two pairs is double, three is triple: shorter and stronger each step.
    • Build Lewis structures from the rules: count valence, lay singles, spread lone pairs, promote where atoms fall short.

    Five chapters back: where does an electron live. Answer: a probability cloud, three quantum numbers. Then Pauli and Hund seated them, shielding ordered them, the seating chart became the table, valence explained the columns, the energy well explained bonding, one continuum collapsed the bond zoo, and valence became a notation.

    One rule did all of it: electrons go to the lowest energy available. Constraints change; the rule doesn't. That is why this is one course, not five.
