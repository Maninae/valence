    Building molecules

    Valence electrons get a piece of paper. The dots become a notation; the notation becomes a tool. With it you can sit down and build real molecules — water, ammonia, methane, the air you breathe.

    Lewis structures octets covalent bonds
    ← Prev: One continuum, not three bonds All chapters →

    Chapter 3 told you which electrons matter: the outermost ones. Chapter 4 told you what they do: share, or tug. This chapter is the bookkeeping. A clean notation for valence electrons turns into a hands-on builder, and the builder lets you assemble the molecules that make up everything you drink, smell, and breathe.

    5.1 Dots for the outermost

    The electrons that do chemistry are the valence electrons: the ones in the outer $s$ and $p$ subshells. Everything else is buried too deep to matter for bonding. Gilbert Lewis, in 1916, proposed a notation as compact as the idea: write the element's symbol, then draw one dot for each valence electron around it. Top, right, bottom, left — one at each side first, then double up. Nothing else.

    Notice the columns line up with valence count. Every alkali metal gets one dot; every halogen gets seven. Lewis's notation is just the periodic table's group number, drawn.

    5.2 Eight is full

    Why does a row of the table end at eight elements? Because a shell's outer layer is exactly one $s$ subshell plus one $p$ subshell: $1 + 3 = 4$ orbitals, and Pauli says two electrons per orbital, so $4 \times 2 = 8$ electrons. Eight is the maximum number of valence electrons a shell can hold — the noble-gas state. Chapter 3 told you the nobles sit at the right edge of each row because their outer shell is full. This is what "full" means.

    Every atom wants that state for the same reason it always has: it's the lowest energy available. So atoms bond by sharing or transferring electrons until each one can count eight. (Hydrogen is the one exception: shell 1 has only the $1s$ orbital, so its full shell is two, not eight. A duet, not an octet.)

    The octet rule

    Main-group atoms bond so that each one ends up surrounded by enough electrons to count a full outer shell: eight for everything past hydrogen, two for hydrogen.

    Here is the trick that makes bonding work. A shared pair belongs to both atoms at once for counting purposes. Neither atom owns those two electrons outright, but each one gets to add them to its own total. That is what a covalent bond is, on paper.

    5.3 Lines are pairs

    Two dots between atoms means a shared pair, which means a bond. Drawing the same thing over and over is tedious, so chemists replaced the shared dots with a line: one line = one shared pair = one bond. Pairs that stay on a single atom (unshared) are still drawn as two dots, side by side. Those are called lone pairs.

    Two worked examples are worth a thousand rules. The recipe is the same every time: count the total valence electrons in the molecule; arrange the atoms with the least-electronegative one in the center (hydrogen is never central, since its full shell is only two); draw single bonds to start; put any leftover electrons as lone pairs on the outer atoms first, then on the center; if anyone is short of eight, promote a single bond to a double or triple by sliding a lone pair into the gap.

    Worked example: water (H₂O)

    Oxygen brings 6 valence electrons; each hydrogen brings 1. Total = 8. Oxygen sits in the middle. Two O–H single bonds use 4 electrons. Oxygen now counts 4 (two shared pairs). The remaining 4 electrons go on oxygen as two lone pairs. Each H counts 2 (its duet). Oxygen counts $2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8$. Done.

    Worked example: ammonia (NH₃)

    Nitrogen brings 5, each hydrogen brings 1. Total = 8. Nitrogen sits in the middle. Three N–H single bonds use 6 electrons. Two left: a single lone pair on nitrogen. Each H counts 2. Nitrogen counts $2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8$. Done.

    5.4 The builder

    Time to do it yourself. The molecule's atoms are pre-arranged in a sensible skeleton. You decide the bonds and the lone pairs. Click between two adjacent atoms to cycle the bond order ($0 \to 1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 0$). Click an atom to add a lone pair (it cycles through $0, 1, 2, 3, 4$ pairs, then wraps).

    The side panel keeps score. Each atom glows green when its count reaches its target shell (8, or 2 for hydrogen) and rose if you overshoot. Use all the electrons, satisfy every atom, and the molecule is done. Hint reveals one nudge; reset clears the board. The ladder unlocks one molecule at a time.

    Why it works

    Nothing in this builder is invented. Each click is moving electrons between two accounts: "shared with a neighbor" or "kept as a lone pair." The win condition is just the octet rule applied honestly to every atom. The fact that you can reach a valid answer for every real molecule in the ladder is the whole reason chemistry has a notation at all.

    5.5 Double and triple are shorter and stronger

    CO₂, O₂, and N₂ force you to draw double and triple bonds. They aren't a separate kind of bond — just more shared pairs in the same gap. More shared pairs means more glue between the same two nuclei, which means the nuclei can sit closer without repelling, and the bond is harder to break.     Triple bonds are why nitrogen gas, $\text{N}_2$, is famously inert. Pulling two nitrogen atoms apart costs 945 kJ/mol — almost three times the energy of an O–H single bond. Roughly four-fifths of the air around you is N₂, and almost none of it ever reacts with anything at room temperature. The whole atmosphere is held in place by a triple bond.

    5.6 What the dots can't say

    Lewis structures are flat bookkeeping. They are right about which atoms are bonded and how many pairs sit where. They are silent about everything else. They don't tell you that methane's four C–H bonds aim outward to the corners of a tetrahedron, not a square. They don't tell you that water is bent at about 105° instead of straight, or why that bend gives water its whole personality. They don't tell you that water molecules stick to each other through tiny extra attractions called hydrogen bonds, and that hydrogen bonds are why ice floats, why your DNA holds its shape, why your blood is liquid at body temperature.

    Geometry and stickiness live behind the door the notation got you to. Chapter 5 stops here; the next site picks up there.

    Coming in v2

    VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) turns the dots into 3D shapes by treating each pair as a balloon that pushes the others away. Polarity (chapter 4) plus geometry decides whether a molecule has a net dipole, which decides whether it dissolves in water, which decides most of biology. Hydrogen bonding follows.

    5.7 What you now have

    • Lewis dot symbols: the valence count drawn around the element's symbol, dots placed singly before they pair (Hund's old rule, in ink).
    • The octet (or duet for hydrogen): one $s$ plus three $p$ orbitals, capped by Pauli, equals an outer shell of eight electrons — the noble-gas state every atom slides toward.
    • A bond is a shared pair both atoms count. Two pairs is a double, three is a triple; each step is shorter and stronger.
    • The Lewis structure of a small molecule is recoverable from rules alone: count valence, lay out single bonds, distribute the rest as lone pairs, promote bonds where atoms come up short.

    Five chapters back, the question was "where does an electron live?" The answer was a probability cloud labeled by three quantum numbers. From there: Pauli and Hund seated the electrons; shielding picked the order; the seating chart unfolded into the periodic table; valence electrons explained why columns share chemistry; the energy well of two atoms meeting explained bonding; one continuum (covalent to polar to ionic) collapsed the bond zoo to a single axis; and the same valence electrons became a working notation for assembling molecules.

    All of it was one rule, applied honestly at every step: electrons go to the lowest energy available, given the constraints. The constraints change — one nucleus, then many electrons, then many atoms — but the rule does not. That is what made this whole thing a single course instead of five.
