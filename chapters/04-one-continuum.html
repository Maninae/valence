    Chapter 4

    One continuum, not three bonds

    Textbooks teach three kinds of bond — covalent, polar, ionic — as if they were three separate phenomena. They aren't. There is one phenomenon on a dial, and the dial is the electronegativity difference between the atoms.

    bonding electronegativity ionic crystal
    ← The table is a map Building molecules →

    Atoms stick together because shared territory lowers their energy. The sharing can be even or lopsided, and in the extreme the cloud detaches and the two atoms become ions. The whole story is one continuous parameter, and this chapter walks it from end to end.

    4.1 Why atoms stick at all

    Bring two hydrogen atoms close together. From far away they barely notice each other. As their probability clouds begin to overlap, an electron sitting between the two nuclei is now pulled by both protons at once: shared territory is cheap territory, energetically. Push the nuclei too close and the picture inverts — the two positive nuclei start repelling each other directly, and the electron clouds get crushed into too small a space. In between is a sweet spot.

    Definition

    The bond energy is the depth of that well: how much you'd have to pay to rip the two atoms back apart. The bond length is where the well's floor sits: the equilibrium separation. For H–H the floor is at $r \approx 0.74$ Å and the depth is $\approx 4.5$ eV. Every "bond" in chemistry is a well of this shape, just with different floor and depth.

    4.2 Sharing, in wave language

    Chapter 1 set up a payoff for later: the electron wave has a sign, and when two waves overlap, signs decide whether they reinforce or cancel. The shared cloud that lowers the energy in section 4.1 is exactly that reinforcement. Two atomic waves with the same sign in the overlap region add to a larger amplitude there; squaring gives extra probability density between the nuclei — exactly where an extra electron pays the lowest price.

    Interactive

    Two clouds, one bond

    Two 1s clouds overlap in phase. The midline density is the shared pair: that's a covalent bond.

    same-sign overlap → extra density between the nuclei → the bond
    Definition

    A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons sitting in a region of overlap between two atoms. "Shared" here means literal: the same two electrons spend time around both nuclei, and the cloud between them carries the extra density.

    4.3 The tug-of-war

    H–H is the symmetric case: both atoms pull the shared cloud equally. Most bonds aren't symmetric. Chapter 3 introduced electronegativity: a number on each atom measuring how greedily it pulls on shared electrons. When the two atoms in a bond have different electronegativities, the cloud sits closer to the hungrier atom. Push the difference high enough and the cloud detaches entirely.

    Note

    The conventional cutoffs — $\Delta\chi < 0.4$ "nonpolar covalent", $0.4$–$1.7$ "polar covalent", $> 1.7$–$2.0$ "ionic" — are conventions, not physics. The cloud morphs smoothly; the labels are just regions we drew on the dial. The continuum bar uses fuzzy borders to say exactly that.

    4.4 The ionic end, in bulk

    When the cloud detaches completely, the two atoms become a positive ion and a negative ion. Now an important fact: a single Na$^+$ and a single Cl$^-$ don't pair up. Each ion's charge pulls on every oppositely-charged neighbor, in all directions, with the same kind of $1/r^2$ attraction. There's no reason to bind to just one partner. So ions stack: every Na$^+$ surrounds itself with as many Cl$^-$ neighbors as fit, and vice versa. The result is a crystal.

    Interactive

    The rock-salt lattice

    Drag to rotate. Every Na⁺ sits in a cage of six Cl⁻, and vice versa. "NaCl molecule" is the wrong unit — the crystal is.

    cubic NaCl — each ion sits at the center of six oppositely-charged neighbors

    Two consequences fall out for free. Salt is brittle: if you shear one layer half a unit cell, suddenly like charges face like charges and the crystal cleaves cleanly along that plane. Salt dissolves in water because each ion can swap its crystal neighbors for a shell of water molecules whose own partial charges do the same job (a topic for a future chapter). And there's no such thing as one NaCl molecule walking around: the unit of matter here is the crystal, not a pair.

    Key insight

    Chemistry's three "kinds" of bond are three regions of one axis. Asking "is this bond covalent or ionic?" is like asking whether 1.9 is small or big. There's a smooth dial. The position on it is set by one number you already know how to compute: $\Delta\chi$.

    4.6 What you now have

    • Atoms bind because shared electrons lower the energy. The minimum of the potential-energy curve is the bond: depth = bond energy, position = bond length.
    • "Shared" means wave overlap in phase: extra probability density between the nuclei. That is a covalent bond.
    • If the two atoms have different electronegativities, the cloud sits closer to the hungrier one. Big enough $\Delta\chi$ and the cloud detaches: you have ions.
    • Ions don't pair up; their attractions point everywhere, so they form crystals. NaCl is a lattice, not a molecule.
    • Covalent, polar, ionic are three names for three regions of one continuous axis: $\Delta\chi$.
