    Chapter 4

    One continuum, not three bonds

    Covalent, polar, ionic: one phenomenon, one dial — the electronegativity difference between the atoms.

    bonding electronegativity ionic crystal
    ← The table is a map Building molecules →

    Atoms stick because shared territory lowers energy. Sharing runs even to lopsided; at the extreme, the cloud detaches into ions. One axis.

    4.1 Why atoms stick at all

    Bring two hydrogens close. An electron between the nuclei feels both protons; shared territory is cheap. Too close, the nuclei repel. Between, a minimum.

    Interactive

    The hydrogen energy well

    Drag the separation. The curve's minimum is the bond.

    readout r = 2.40 Å    E = −0.02 eV
    Definition

    Bond energy = well depth (cost to pull apart). Bond length = floor position. For H–H, $r \approx 0.74$ Å, depth $\approx 4.5$ eV. Every bond has this shape; only floor and depth change.

    4.2 Sharing, in wave language

    From chapter 1: electron waves carry a sign; signs decide whether overlap reinforces or cancels. Same-sign waves add; squaring gives extra density between the nuclei — where an electron pays least.

    Interactive

    Two clouds, one bond

    Two 1s clouds overlap in phase. The midline density is the bond.

    same-sign overlap → extra density between the nuclei → the bond
    Definition

    A covalent bond is a shared electron pair in the overlap: one cloud, two nuclei.

    4.3 The tug-of-war

    H–H pulls symmetrically. Most bonds don't. Chapter 3's electronegativity is each atom's pull on shared electrons. Unequal pulls skew the cloud toward the hungrier atom; far enough, it detaches.

    Note

    The cutoffs ($\Delta\chi < 0.4$ nonpolar, $0.4$–$1.7$ polar, $> 1.7$ ionic) are conventions — labels on a smooth dial, not physics.

    4.4 The ionic end, in bulk

    Detached, the atoms are ions. Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ don't pair off: each charge pulls every opposite neighbor by the same $1/r^2$ force. No preferred partner, so ions stack — every Na$^+$ ringed by Cl$^-$. A crystal.

    Interactive

    The rock-salt lattice

    Drag to rotate. Every Na⁺ sits in a cage of six Cl⁻. The crystal is the unit, not a molecule.

    cubic NaCl — each ion centered on six opposite neighbors

    Two free consequences. Brittle: shear a layer half a cell and like charges meet; it cleaves. Dissolves in water: each ion swaps crystal neighbors for water molecules with matching partial charges.

    Key insight

    "Covalent or ionic?" is like "is 1.9 big?" — a smooth dial, one number: $\Delta\chi$.

    4.6 What you now have

    • Shared electrons lower energy. The minimum is the bond: depth = energy, position = length.
    • In-phase overlap → extra density between the nuclei: a covalent bond.
    • Unequal pulls skew the cloud. Big enough $\Delta\chi$, it detaches: ions.
    • Ions pull every direction → crystals, not pairs.
    • Covalent, polar, ionic: three regions of one axis.
