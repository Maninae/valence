    Key insight

    The wide middle is the 4s/3d crossing made geometric. Periods 1–3 have no d block because no d subshell has filled. At scandium ($Z=21$), 3d drops low enough and ten elements land. Lanthanides and actinides repeat the story for $f$.

    3.2 Blocks are subshells

    Each colored region is one subshell filling. Subshells have fixed capacities (1 $s$, 3 $p$, 5 $d$, 7 $f$ orbitals; 2 electrons each), so block widths do too.

    The period number is the outer shell's $n$. 3d sits in period 4 because it fills during the period when 4s has just opened. Counting rows is counting shells.

    3.3 Columns are families

    A column shares the same outer configuration at higher $n$. Lithium $[\text{He}]\,2s^1$, sodium $[\text{Ne}]\,3s^1$, potassium $[\text{Ar}]\,4s^1$: one $s$ electron outside a closed shell. Same reactive part, same chemistry.

    Definition

    Valence electrons occupy the outer shell (highest $n$, $s$ and $p$) and do the bonding. The core sits too deep to react; it just shields.

    Alkali metals: one electron beyond a noble-gas core, easy to lose. Halogens ($ns^2 np^5$): one short of a full shell, they grab. Noble gases ($ns^2 np^6$): full shell, barely react.

    3.4 One number drives the map

    $Z_{\text{eff}}$, the effective nuclear charge, is the pull an outer electron feels after shielding: $Z$ minus a shielding number. It does almost all the explaining.

    Across a period, each step adds a proton and an electron to the same shell. Same-shell electrons shield poorly, so $Z$ climbs faster than shielding. $Z_{\text{eff}}$ rises; the cloud tightens: smaller, harder to ionize, hungrier in bonds.

    Down a column, valence sits in a farther shell over a full inner one. Shielding jumps, distance grows: bigger cloud, looser outer electron.

    Definition

    Electronegativity ($\chi$, Pauling scale) is how hard an atom pulls on shared electrons. High $\chi$ (fluorine 3.98, oxygen 3.44) drags them in; low $\chi$ (cesium 0.79, sodium 0.93) lets go. It is $Z_{\text{eff}}$ in another hat.

    Looking ahead

    Electronegativity predicts whether a bond looks covalent, polar, or ionic. Chapter 4 makes it one spectrum, governed by $\Delta\chi$.

    3.5 What you now have

    • Block widths = subshell sizes ($s{:}2$, $p{:}6$, $d{:}10$, $f{:}14$). Period number = outer shell's $n$.
    • Columns share outer configuration. Alkalis lose one; halogens grab one; noble gases sit content.
    • Valence electrons (outer $s$, $p$) do chemistry; cores just shield.
    • $Z_{\text{eff}}$ rises right, drops down. Atoms shrink right, grow down; IE and $\chi$ track it.
    • $\chi$ drives chapter 4.
