    The table is a map

    The periodic table is not a chart to memorize. Its weird shape is the filling order made visible, and one number — effective nuclear charge — drives every trend across it.

    blocks valence Zeff trends
    ← The filling order One continuum, not three bonds →

    Chapter 2 gave you the filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, and so on. This chapter takes that one rule and lets it draw the periodic table for you, block by block. Once the table exists, you'll read three trends — size, ionization energy, electronegativity — off the same number you already know: Zeff.

    3.1 Build it, don’t memorize it

    The filling order is a recipe. Hand it to a printer with one rule — "drop each new element into the next empty slot, lined up by the subshell it lands in" — and the printer draws the periodic table. The shape is not a design choice. It is the recipe, frozen.

    Press play. Each element appears in turn, colored by the subshell whose electron just got added. Watch the s block (2 wide) and p block (6 wide) fill periods 2 and 3 by themselves, then watch the d block arrive in period 4 — ten elements wide, ten elements late.

    Key insight

    The table's weird wide middle is chapter 2's 4s/3d crossing made geometric. Periods 1–3 have no d block because no d subshell has filled yet. By scandium ($Z=21$) the 3d energy has finally dropped enough to fill — and a whole decade of elements drops into the middle. The lanthanides and actinides (the floating strip at the bottom) are the same story for $f$.

    3.2 Blocks are subshells

    Each colored region of the table is one subshell type filling. Subshells come in fixed sizes (chapter 1: one $s$, three $p$, five $d$, seven $f$ orbitals; two electrons per orbital), so block widths come in fixed sizes too. The table is to scale.

    And the rows are not arbitrary either. The period number (1, 2, 3, …) is the outermost shell's $n$. Period 4 is the $n=4$ shell coming online; the 3d block that lives in period 4 is there because its electrons fill during the period when 4s has just opened. Counting rows is counting shells.

    3.3 Columns are families

    Elements in the same column have the same outer-shell configuration, just stacked at a higher $n$. Lithium is $[\text{He}]\,2s^1$; sodium is $[\text{Ne}]\,3s^1$; potassium is $[\text{Ar}]\,4s^1$. All three have one lonely $s$ electron orbiting outside a closed shell. They react the same way because the part of the atom doing the chemistry is the same.

    Definition

    The valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell — the highest $n$ — specifically its $s$ and $p$ occupants. They are the electrons an atom uses to bond. Inner shells (called the core) sit too deep to interact with other atoms; they just sit there shielding the nucleus. Chemistry is a story about valence electrons.

    Three families tell the rest of the story already. Alkali metals have one electron beyond a noble-gas core: it sits far out, shielded from the nucleus by everything below, easy to peel off. So alkalis are violently eager to lose that one electron. Halogens are one electron short of a full outer shell ($ns^2 np^5$, missing one $p$ slot): they grab. Noble gases have a full outer shell ($ns^2 np^6$): nothing to give, no need to take. They barely react.

    3.4 One number drives the map

    In chapter 2 we built $Z_{\text{eff}}$, the effective nuclear charge: the nuclear pull an outer electron actually feels after inner electrons screen it. It is not the proton count $Z$ — it is the proton count minus a shielding number. Across a row of the table, that one number does almost all the explaining.

    Move right along a period. Each new element adds one proton ($Z\uparrow$) and one electron to the same outer shell. Same-shell electrons are bad at shielding each other (they orbit at similar distances), so the shielding barely budges while $Z$ climbs. $Z_{\text{eff}}$ rises across the row. The outer cloud gets pulled tighter to the nucleus — smaller, harder to ionize, hungrier in bonds.

    Move down a column. Each new period puts the valence electrons in a whole new shell, farther out, with a full extra inner shell beneath them. Shielding jumps, distance grows. The cloud gets bigger and the outer electron easier to give up.

    Definition

    Electronegativity ($\chi$, on the Pauling scale) is how strongly an atom pulls on the electrons it is sharing with another atom in a bond. High $\chi$ atoms (fluorine 3.98, oxygen 3.44) drag shared electrons toward themselves; low $\chi$ atoms (cesium 0.79, sodium 0.93) give them up easily. The pattern is just $Z_{\text{eff}}$ wearing a different hat: tighter grip on your own electrons means tighter grip on borrowed ones too.

    Looking ahead

    Electronegativity is the only number you need to predict whether a bond looks covalent, polar, or ionic. Chapter 4 is one continuous spectrum, controlled by $\Delta\chi$ between the two atoms in the bond. The map you just read sets that up.

    3.5 What you now have

    • The periodic table's shape is the filling order made visible. Block widths are subshell sizes ($s{:}2$, $p{:}6$, $d{:}10$, $f{:}14$); period number is the outermost shell's $n$.
    • Columns are families: same outer configuration, same chemistry. Alkali metals lose one electron eagerly; halogens grab one; noble gases sit content.
    • The valence electrons — outer $s$ and $p$ — do the chemistry. Cores just shield.
    • $Z_{\text{eff}}$ climbs right (same shell, less shielding gain per proton) and drops down (new shell, more screen): atoms shrink right, grow down; ionization energy and electronegativity rise right, fall down.
    • Electronegativity ($\chi$) is the next chapter's engine.
