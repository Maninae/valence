    The filling order

    Orbitals are seats; electrons are an audience that has to sit. Three rules govern the seating, and the seats themselves shift as the nucleus grows heavier. That shift, more than any rule, explains the entire transition row.

    Pauli Hund aufbau shielding

    Chapter 1 built the seats. This chapter seats the audience. You will play the filling game by hand, then watch the marquee chart of subshell energies scrolling against atomic number — and see why 4s fills before 3d at potassium, yet vanishes first when iron rusts.

    2.1 Two electrons per orbital

    Electrons carry a built-in two-valued property called spin. It is not a literal rotation — it is an intrinsic quantum number that takes exactly two values, written $\uparrow$ and $\downarrow$. The Pauli exclusion principle says no two electrons in an atom can share the exact same quantum state. Since an orbital fixes $(n, \ell, m)$, two electrons can share it only if their spins are opposite:

    ↑ ↓ one orbital, two seats, opposite spins

    Every orbital fits two electrons. Period. Counting: one s orbital → 2 electrons; three p orbitals → 6; five d → 10; seven f → 14. Those numbers are the widths of the blocks of the periodic table, but that is chapter 3.

    2.2 Shielding breaks the tie

    In a hydrogen atom — one proton, one electron — energy depends only on $n$. The 2s and 2p subshells are degenerate: same energy, no preference. Add electrons and that symmetry breaks.

    An outer electron does not feel the bare nuclear charge $Z$. Inner electrons sit between it and the nucleus and partially cancel that pull. The outer electron sees an effective charge:

    $$Z_\mathrm{eff} = Z - \htmlData{tip=The shielding constant — how much pull the inner electrons cancel}{S}$$

    Now chapter 1's penetration matters. Within a shell, s orbitals dip deepest into the core cloud and feel close to the full nuclear pull; p dips less; d barely at all. So $Z_\mathrm{eff}$ differs by subshell, and:

    Within one shell

    $E_{ns} < E_{np} < E_{nd} < E_{nf}$ — the more an orbital penetrates the core, the more nucleus it feels, and the lower (more negative) its energy.

    Diagram

    Why s sees more nucleus than d

    An outer s electron pokes inside the inner-electron cloud and feels the bare nucleus. A d electron sits outside, feels a screened, weakened pull.

    2.3 The seating rules

    Three rules, together, decide how electrons fill the orbitals of a ground-state atom. State them once; then play.

    • Aufbau ("building up"): each electron takes the lowest open seat.
    • Pauli: at most two electrons per orbital, opposite spins.
    • Hund: within a subshell of equal orbitals, spread out before pairing up. Same-spin electrons in different orbitals avoid each other; pairing costs energy.

    2.4 The rungs move

    The ladder in section 2.3 looks fixed. It is not. Subshell energies depend on $Z$, and they shift at different rates. The 4s curve dives gently as $Z$ grows; the 3d curve dives much faster once it starts to fill. Somewhere between potassium and scandium, the curves cross.

    About the model

    Energies plotted below come from Slater's rules, an empirical approximation for $Z_\mathrm{eff}$ first published in 1930. It is not the truth — real atoms need numerical Hartree–Fock — but it captures the crossings and the trends honestly with one page of arithmetic. Treat the chart as a faithful model of a real effect.

    Key insight

    The aufbau order is not a list to memorize. Every electron takes the lowest seat available at the time it arrives, and the seats themselves shift as $Z$ grows. At $Z=19$ the cheapest open seat is 4s. By $Z=21$ that same seat is more expensive than 3d. Same physics, different answer.

    2.5 Last in, first out — except

    Here is a fact that has puzzled chemistry students for a century: iron, when it forms Fe2+, loses both of its 4s electrons before it touches 3d. But we just said 4s fills first. So why does it leave first too?

    Look at the chart from 2.4 around iron ($Z=26$). The 3d curve has plunged. 3d sits deep below 4s — basically core. The highest-energy electrons in neutral iron are the 4s pair, and ionization removes the highest-energy electrons first. The seat 4s was cheap when iron was filling; it became expensive once iron was full.

    2.6 The exceptions prove the rule

    Two elements in the first transition row refuse to follow the seating game:

    • Chromium ($Z=24$): not [Ar] 3d4 4s2, but [Ar] 3d5 4s1.
    • Copper ($Z=29$): not [Ar] 3d9 4s2, but [Ar] 3d10 4s1.

    Near $Z=24$ and $Z=29$, 4s and 3d are very close in energy. When two subshells are nearly degenerate, a smaller effect can break the tie: electrons in a half-filled or fully-filled d set keep out of each other's way better, lowering total repulsion. Promoting one 4s electron to 3d gives Cr a half-filled d (five same-spin electrons, all in different orbitals — Hund happy) and gives Cu a complete d shell. The energy saved on repulsion is worth more than the energy paid to climb.

    The real rule

    Atoms minimize total energy, not "energy per orbital". The aufbau game we played in 2.3 is the right answer when subshell gaps are large. When they are small, repulsion between paired electrons gets a vote, and occasionally that vote tips the result. The exceptions confirm that the underlying law is energy minimization, not a fixed seating chart.

    2.7 What you now have

    • Pauli caps every orbital at two electrons of opposite spin.
    • Shielding plus penetration breaks the $n$-only degeneracy of hydrogen: within a shell, $E_{ns} < E_{np} < E_{nd}$.
    • Aufbau + Hund + Pauli predicts the ground-state configuration of every atom you tried.
    • Subshell energies move with $Z$; the 4s/3d crossing between Ca and Sc explains both why 4s fills first and why transition metals ionize from 4s.
    • Cr and Cu cheat the chart because the underlying law is total energy, not the chart.
